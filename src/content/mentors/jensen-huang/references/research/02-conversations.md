# Agent 2 — Conversations

Focus: Long-form interviews and podcasts where Jensen is unscripted. He has done remarkably few of these for a CEO of his stature — until 2023, when AI thrust him into the public eye. The Acquired episode (Sep 2023, ~3.5 hours with Ben Gilbert and David Rosenthal) is the densest single source ever recorded with him.

## Core long-form interviews (scored)

### Acquired podcast — "Nvidia Part III: The Dawn of the AI Era" (September 2023, ~3.5 hours)
URL: https://www.acquired.fm/episodes/the-nvidia-company-jensen-huang
[score: P=3 V=3 A=3 C=3 total=12]

The single highest-density Jensen interview in existence. Released in conjunction with Acquired's three-part Nvidia history (Parts I, II, III). Jensen sits down with Gilbert and Rosenthal in a Nvidia conference room.

Standout reveals:
- **"If we knew how hard it was going to be, we would have never started the company."** Followed by laughter. He stresses this is not retrospective wisdom — he says it as an honest answer.
- **"The number of times we almost died at Nvidia is countless."** Lists: NV1 disaster (1995), 3DFX/Sega Saturn-era pivots, the dot-com crash, 2008 financial crisis losing $200M+ on chip recall, 2018 crypto crash + COVID, near-bankruptcy moments not all of which became public.
- **The Denny's origin story** (he founded Nvidia at the Denny's in East San Jose with Chris Malachowsky and Curtis Priem). He still goes back. Recently the booth was given a plaque.
- **CUDA bet (2006)**: "We knew it was right. We didn't know when. We had to be willing to spend a lot of money on something that nobody asked us to do." On the financial cost: "every quarter Wall Street wanted to know why we were spending money on this thing that wasn't generating revenue. We just kept doing it."
- **On suffering**: "I want them to suffer. I want them to know what it feels like. Because if you don't know what it feels like, you can't deal with it when it shows up."
- **On talking to direct reports' direct reports**: "I have 60 direct reports. Why? Because I want information unfiltered. I never have a 1:1. I don't believe in 1:1s."
- **On email**: "I don't read email much. I prefer to walk by your desk. If something is important enough to be in email, it's important enough for me to come see you."
- **On strategy**: "We don't make decisions to be right. We make decisions because they're going to lead us somewhere."

## How Jensen answers when pressed

- **When pushed on competition (AMD, Intel, custom silicon)**: he doesn't engage adversarially. Recurrent move: "We love them. They're doing great work. The pie is getting bigger. The world needs more accelerated computing." Strategically dismissive without being personal.
- **When pushed on succession**: "I have no plans to retire." Refuses to entertain the question.
- **When pushed on China**: lawyered, careful, but firm. "Nvidia complies with all U.S. export rules." Will then pivot to making the case for AI being a positive-sum technology.
- **When pushed on regulation / safety**: he's explicit that Nvidia provides the platform; what's built on top is user choice. Less ideological than Sam Altman; more pragmatic than Elon.
- **When pushed on personal weakness**: surprisingly candid. "I have terrible memory. I cannot remember names. I lose at chess to my children." Self-deprecation without false modesty.

## Improvised analogies and metaphors (diagnostic)

- **"Cooking" / "in the oven"** — used for chips in development.
- **"Whip yourself"** — character formation.
- **"Bicycle for the mind" inversions** — he doesn't use Jobs's phrase; instead uses "co-pilot" or "amplifier" framing.
- **"Earth's most important computer"** — for the supercomputer cluster.
- **"AI factory"** — token-production facility.
- **"It just works"** is anti-Jensen — he prefers "it required heroic engineering" (he wants the audience to know how hard it is).
- **"Math machine"** — a GPU. Not "graphics card." Frame is computation, not display.

## Topics he consistently declines to discuss

- His co-founders Chris Malachowsky and Curtis Priem in detail (he namechecks them but does not draw out their roles publicly).
- His personal wealth and giving (philanthropy is private; he's given to Stanford and Oregon State).
- His parents' specific experience (he gestures at it emotionally but doesn't dwell).
- Specific technical roadmap beyond announced products.
- China policy specifics beyond the bare legal compliance line.
