# Agent 2 — Long-form Conversations of Steve Jobs

## Summary

Steve Jobs's thinking is sharpest in three settings: (1) extended pre-ouster interviews where he is unguarded and ideologically loaded — **Playboy 1985 (David Sheff)** is the densest single primary source [score: P=3 V=3 A=3 C=3 total=12]; (2) **wilderness-era candor (1994–1996)**, especially the **1995 PBS "Lost Interview" with Bob Cringely** (originally for *Triumph of the Nerds*) [score: P=3 V=3 A=3 C=3 total=12], the **1995 Smithsonian Oral History with Daniel Morrow** [score: P=3 V=3 A=3 C=3 total=12], the **1994 Rolling Stone interview with Jeff Goodell** [score: P=3 V=3 A=3 C=3 total=12], and the **Feb 1996 Wired interview with Gary Wolf** [score: P=3 V=3 A=3 C=3 total=12]; and (3) **late-career on-stage settings** — the **D5 (2007) joint interview with Bill Gates** and the **D8 (2010) solo interview**, both transcribed by AllThingsD [score: P=3 V=3 A=3 C=3 total=12]. The 1996 Rolling Stone-style interviews are where Jobs is most analytic about other companies; the 2010 D8 is where he is most reflective about Apple's own choices. The 1985 Playboy and the 1995 Lost Interview are the two highest-yield primary documents because both run long enough that Jobs exhausts his rehearsed lines and starts thinking in real time.

## Recurring Stances (≥ 3 distinct interviews)

**Stance 1: Computers are tools that amplify innate human capability ("bicycle for the mind").**
- Evidence: 1990 Memory & Imagination film [score: P=3 V=3 A=3 C=3 total=12]: "humans are tool builders … a man on a bicycle blew the condor away … that's what a computer is to me." Repeated in Playboy 1985: "A computer is the most incredible tool we've ever seen." Repeated in 1995 Lost Interview: "Humans are tool builders, and we build tools that can dramatically amplify our innate human abilities."
- Evolution: Held steady across 25+ years. The framing is identical from 1985 to 2010 — only the device changes (Mac → NeXT → iPhone → iPad).

**Stance 2: Marketing-driven companies rot from within; product-driven companies decay when sales people displace product people.**
- Evidence: Lost Interview 1995 [P=3 V=3 A=3 C=3 total=12]: "The product sensibility and product genius that brought them to a monopolistic position gets rotted out by people running these companies who have no conception of a good product." Echoed Playboy 1985 (re: IBM): "There's been virtually zero innovation since IBM got dominant control." Echoed in Rolling Stone 1994 re: Microsoft.
- Evolution: Steady. The IBM/Xerox version becomes the Microsoft version becomes the implicit Google version by 2010.

**Stance 3: A-players self-select; B-players contaminate.**
- Evidence: Lost Interview 1995: "The difference between average and the best in software is 50-to-1, maybe 100-to-1 … When you get enough A players together, they don't want to work with B and C players." Smithsonian 1995 [score: P=3 V=2 A=3 C=2 total=10] (corroborated by Isaacson biography). Echoed at D8 2010 implicitly via "great products need great people and great focus" [P=2 V=3 A=3 C=2 total=10].
- Evolution: Held steady but softened in tone post-2000 — the bozo language largely disappears from on-stage interviews.

**Stance 4: Faith is in people, not tools.**
- Evidence: Rolling Stone 1994 [P=3 V=3 A=3 C=3 total=12]: "It's not the tools that you have faith in — tools are just tools. They work, or they don't work. It's people you have faith in or not." Playboy 1985: "We think that people are basically tool users. So if we can just get lots of computers to lots of people, it will make some qualitative difference in the world." Lost Interview 1995 (process vs content).
- Evolution: Steady. Note tension with Stance 1 — tools amplify, but only because the people are the source.

**Stance 5: The carpenter-and-the-back-of-the-cabinet — quality on hidden surfaces.**
- Evidence: Playboy 1985: "When you're a carpenter making a beautiful chest of drawers, you're not going to use a piece of plywood on the back, even though it faces the wall and nobody will ever see it." Repeated by Jobs at numerous internal Apple meetings (corroborated Isaacson 2011, Hertzfeld *Folklore*) [P=2 V=3 A=3 C=3 total=11]. Echoed in 1995 Lost Interview re: craftsmanship between idea and product.
- Evolution: Held steady. This is one of his oldest signature framings.

## Diagnostic Analogies and Metaphors

- **"Bicycle for the mind"** — 1990 Memory & Imagination film. Origin: a *Scientific American* article on locomotion efficiency. Jobs's most cited analogy for what a computer is. [P=3 V=3 A=3 C=3 total=12]
- **"It's as significant as the difference between the telephone and the telegraph"** — Playboy 1985, deflecting "expensive Etch A Sketch" mockery of the Mac. [P=3 V=3 A=3 C=2 total=11]
- **"Two planets in their own orbits that every so often intersect"** — Playboy 1985, on his relationship with Wozniak. [P=3 V=3 A=3 C=2 total=11]
- **"The Macintosh was sort of like this wonderful romance in your life that you once had — and that produced about 10 million children"** — Rolling Stone 1994. Romantic-biological hybrid metaphor for product legacy. [P=3 V=3 A=3 C=2 total=11]
- **"Microsoft … it's McDonald's"** — Lost Interview 1995. Taste-as-cuisine analogy. [P=3 V=3 A=3 C=3 total=12]
- **"Stuck thoughts construct patterns like scaffolding in your mind"** — Playboy 1985, on why people get rigid as they age. [P=3 V=3 A=3 C=2 total=11]
- **"PCs are gonna be like trucks. They were the only kind of car when we were agrarian; once we became urban, most people switched to cars"** — D8 2010. The post-PC framing. [P=3 V=3 A=3 C=3 total=12]
- **"The Beatles … four very talented guys who kept each other's negative tendencies in check. The total was greater than the sum of the parts"** — 60 Minutes 2003 (and repeated ≥5 times across interviews). Jobs's go-to model for company-as-band. [P=3 V=3 A=3 C=3 total=12]
- **"Glide slope to die"** — Lost Interview 1995, on Apple's trajectory under Sculley/Spindler. Aviation imagery for organizational decline. [P=3 V=3 A=3 C=2 total=11]
- **"They want to kill the iPhone. We won't let them"** — D8 2010 on Google. Combat framing. [P=3 V=3 A=3 C=3 total=12]
- **"Things are packages of emphasis"** — D8 2010, defending exclusion of Flash. Compression of his entire focus philosophy. [P=2 V=3 A=3 C=2 total=10]

## Under-Pressure Behavior Patterns

When directly challenged, Jobs uses a small repertoire of moves, observable across decades:

1. **Historical analogy as deflection.** Asked in Playboy 1985 what people would *do* with a $3,000 computer, he answers with Alexander Graham Bell unable to predict telephone use — converting "give me specifics" into "you're being unimaginative." [P=3 V=3 A=3 C=3 total=12]
2. **Reframe the question's premise.** Asked at D5 2007 about the Apple-Microsoft "war," he refuses the framing: "If the game was a zero-sum game where for Apple to win, Microsoft had to lose, then Apple was going to lose." [P=3 V=3 A=3 C=3 total=12]
3. **Concede the small point, retain the large one.** Smithsonian 1995 / Playboy 1985 about Lisa: "We expressed very high hopes for Lisa and we were wrong" — admits failure of execution while preserving the architectural argument. [P=3 V=3 A=3 C=2 total=11]
4. **Hard pivot to silence.** Playboy 1985 on biological parents: "That's one area I really don't want to talk about." End of subject. No bridge, no softening. [P=3 V=3 A=3 C=2 total=11]
5. **Counter-question / Socratic flip.** Multiple D-conference instances; he asks Mossberg or Swisher what *they* mean before answering — observed at D5 2007 and D8 2010. [P=2 V=3 A=3 C=2 total=10]
6. **Long pause then restatement.** D8 2010 on Google "don't be evil": he pauses, then says "It's bullshit" — the pause signals the verdict is considered, not reactive. [P=3 V=3 A=3 C=3 total=12]
7. **Self-deprecating concession (rare, post-2000).** D8 2010: "We never saw ourselves as in a platform war with Microsoft, and maybe that's why we lost" — concedes a strategic error in his own framing. [P=3 V=3 A=3 C=2 total=11]

## Topics Avoided

- **Biological parents** — Playboy 1985, hard refusal. Pattern continues through life (Isaacson confirms). [P=3 V=3 A=3 C=3 total=12]
- **Personal philanthropy** — Playboy 1985: "That's a part of my life that I like to keep private." Jobs is asked variants of this for 25 years and never gives a substantive answer. [P=3 V=3 A=3 C=3 total=12]
- **Specific Apple board / Sculley legal-history details** post-1985 — answers in generalities; named individuals rare in on-record settings post-1996.
- **Health / illness** — after 2004, dodges or gives one-line answers; D8 2010 visibly thinner, no extended health discussion. [P=2 V=3 A=3 C=3 total=11]
- **Steve Jobs's own role / future at Apple after 2008** — repeatedly punts to "I have great people."
- **His daughter Lisa (early life)** — never volunteered in interviews; only addressed when asked.
- **Specific revenue / financial guidance** — predictably refers to CFO; not novel, but a consistent silence.

## Stance Evolution

**On Microsoft.** 1985 Playboy: barely mentioned (IBM is the enemy). 1994 Rolling Stone: "Microsoft has had two goals … to copy the Mac, and to copy Lotus." 1995 Lost Interview: "no taste … McDonald's." 2007 D5: total reframe — "Bill built the first software company in the industry … the relationship is one of our best developer relationships." Jobs publicly de-escalates between 1995 and 2007. [Cross-ref P=3 V=3 A=3 C=3 total=12]

**On the home computer market.** Playboy 1985: "So far, that's more of a conceptual market than a real market" — admits the home use case has not arrived. By 1996 Wired and 2007 D5, he treats consumer computing as obviously real and declines to revisit the earlier hedge.

**On platform / openness.** 1985: Apple-as-different-from-IBM is the whole pitch. 1996 Wired: speaks pessimistically about whether technology can save anything. 2007 D5: explicit endorsement of partnering ("we partner with people that are really good at stuff … it's really hard for one company to do everything"). 2010 D8: returns to integration ("packages of emphasis," excludes Flash). The arc bends toward more pragmatic partnership rhetoric in middle, then re-tightens late.

**On wealth.** Playboy 1985: dismissive, "humorous … makes me feel old when students are most in awe of the millionaire" — implicitly disdainful. By Smithsonian 1995 and Rolling Stone 1994 he is matter-of-fact about it; the 1985 moralism is gone.

**On his own legacy / Apple's permanence.** Playboy 1985: "There may be a few years when I'm not there, but I'll always come back" (uncannily prescient). Rolling Stone 1994: "I don't want to talk about Apple." D5 2007 / D8 2010: openly proud, no hedging. The 1994 silence reads as wound; the post-1997 voice reads as owner.

## Notes & Contradictions

- **Older minds vs experienced managers.** Playboy 1985 he says people get neurologically stuck as they age — yet in the same interview admits experienced managers were right and he was wrong on Lisa. He never resolves this.
- **No market research vs intense user observation.** Playboy 1985: "We weren't going to go out and do market research." Yet biographies (Isaacson) and contemporaneous Apple records show extensive user-testing of the Mac and iMac. The interview claim is rhetorical; the working method was different. [Cross-ref P=2 V=3 A=3 C=3 total=11]
- **Faith in people / 50-to-1 difference.** Stated faith in "people" coexists with belief that nearly all people are not A-players. The democratic populism of Playboy 1985 and the elitism of the Lost Interview 1995 sit in unresolved tension.
- **Microsoft taste claim.** 1995 Lost Interview: Microsoft has "no taste." D5 2007: collegial, generous tone with Gates personally. The contradiction is clearly social-context-driven, not a belief change — when on stage with Gates he picks the magnanimous register; in 1995 alone with Cringely he picks the prophet register.
- **"It doesn't matter, you've got to let go of that stuff"** (D5 2007 on regrets) vs. the still-pointed 1995 description of Sculley destroying his life's work. Public absolution; private grievance.

## Information Sparsity

- **Internal Apple all-hands recordings** are referenced in many secondary sources but transcripts are not consistently public; quotes circulate via Isaacson and Hertzfeld rather than primary recordings.
- **The Smithsonian Oral History 1995** transcript is well-attested as existing (Smithsonian site, Steve Jobs Archive) but the full transcript was not fully retrievable in this research budget — only excerpts. This is the single highest-value gap.
- **Mid-NeXT-period (1988–1993) long-form interviews** are sparse; Jobs gave fewer set-piece interviews during the NeXT hardware years, and the 1991 Fortune (Schlender) is the main bridge between Playboy 1985 and Rolling Stone 1994.
- **Late-life Asian-press interviews** (e.g., NHK 2001) exist but English transcripts are partial.
- **D10 / hypothetical late D-conference appearances** — never happened; D8 2010 is the final long-form on-stage appearance.
- **Charlie Rose 1996 (Pixar/Lasseter)** — substantial but mostly Pixar-focused; less diagnostic of his thinking framework than the Cringely or Sheff interviews.
