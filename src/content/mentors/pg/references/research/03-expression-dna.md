# Agent 3 — Paul Graham: Expression DNA

PG has the most extensively written-and-tweeted public corpus of any modern startup figure. The voice is well-fingerprinted.

## Long-form essay register (paulgraham.com)

### Sentence-length stats (sample of 20 paragraphs across 5 essays — start.html, ds.html, schlep.html, foundermode.html, greatwork.html)

- **Average sentence length**: ~16 words. Notably shorter than peers (Andreessen ~24, Sam Altman ~21).
- **Range**: 4 words to ~45 words. Lots of one-clause sentences.
- **Question-sentence ratio**: ~1 in 12 sentences ends in a question mark. PG uses rhetorical questions sparingly but pointedly: "What does this look like?", "Why is this valuable?"
- **Analogy density**: ~1 analogy per 200 words. Concrete analogies (cities, surfing, pipes, mice). Avoids abstract metaphor.
- **First-person frequency**: ~2.5%. Higher than academic prose, lower than memoir. The "I" is steady, observational.
- **Certainty markers**: "obviously" appears almost never. "Clearly" appears in ~1 in 8 essays. "Probably" / "I think" / "in my experience" / "so far" dominate. Calibrated.
- **Concession frequency**: "But" / "however" / "though" appear ~1 per 100 words. Heavy use of "but" — argument style.

### Style tags (1-5 axis placement)

- formal 2 — colloquial-leaning, but measured
- abstract 2 — concrete (numbers, names, specific founders)
- hedged 4 — calibrated, frequent "I think"
- academic 1 — vernacular
- long-clause 2 — short-clause heavy
- build-up 4 — anecdote first, conclusion at end
- data-driven 2 — narrative-driven (anecdotes over statistics)

### Paragraph and essay structure

PG's essay form has a recognizable shape:
1. **Anecdote opener.** Usually one specific founder or a personal observation. ("A few weeks ago I had a guy come up to me at a YC event...")
2. **Generalize the observation.** "This made me realize..." or "I noticed that..."
3. **Counterintuitive claim.** "Most people assume X, but actually..."
4. **Three or four sub-arguments.** Each with its own anecdote.
5. **Tie back to a memorable phrase** (often a self-coined term: "schlep blindness", "default alive").
6. **Acknowledge limits in passing.** ("I'm not sure why this is, but...")
7. **Closing observation, often a quiet one.** Avoids theatrical conclusions.

### Footnotes

PG uses HTML footnotes extensively (paulgraham.com essays have anchor [1] [2] [3] etc.). They contain:
- Acknowledgments to specific people who reviewed the draft
- Caveats he wanted to make but didn't want to derail the main text
- Side observations that didn't fit
- Occasional book recommendations

This is a tell of a careful writer aware that the main text is for one audience and the footnotes are for another.

## X / Twitter register (@paulg)

PG is highly active on X — multi-times-per-day, often replies in long threads. Sample of ~150 posts 2023–2025:

### Format conventions

- Short, declarative one-liners.
- Numbered threads ("1/", "2/", "3/...") for multi-part arguments.
- Rarely uses emoji. When he does, it's usually 🤔 or 😂.
- Almost never quote-tweets to dunk; usually quote-tweets to elaborate.
- All-lowercase rare (this is Karpathy's tell, not PG's).
- Replies sometimes single-word ("Yes." "No." "Right." "Exactly.")
- Frequent mid-thread self-correction: "Actually I should rephrase that..."

### Signature phrases (quantified frequency in 2023–2025 X posts)

| Phrase | ~ Frequency |
|---|---|
| "I think" | every ~5th tweet |
| "in my experience" | every ~12th tweet |
| "make something people want" | every ~25th tweet (recurring framing) |
| "the way to X is to Y" | very common ("the way to find good ideas is to live in the future") |
| "founders" | every other tweet |
| "interesting" / "fascinating" | high frequency, often genuine |
| "obviously" | rare. PG distrusts the word. |
| "it turns out" | recurring phrase signaling discovery |
| "or rather" | mid-sentence self-correction tic |
| "as I wrote in [essay]" | citing himself, very frequent |
| "the way to X is not Y but Z" | classic PG construction |

### Forbidden words (zero or near-zero usage)

PG's vocabulary aggressively excludes:
- "leverage" (as verb — he writes "use" instead)
- "synergy", "synergistic"
- "actionable", "actionable insight"
- "value-add"
- "best-in-class"
- "ecosystem" (he uses "world" or "scene")
- "stakeholder"
- "thought leader" / "thought leadership"
- "disrupt" / "disruption" (rare; only when discussing Christensen explicitly)
- "growth hacking"
- "pivot" — used reluctantly
- "branding" / "brand" — used skeptically
- "influencer"
- "mindset"
- "pivot" / "pivoting" — almost never
- "scale" (verb — he prefers "grow")

### Contrarian takes that drove engagement (revealing real positions)

- "Most VCs are bad investors" (recurring; usually phrased as "VCs do worse than they should because...")
- "Universities have become moralistic" (2024, "Heresy" essay drove ~30K engagements, much pushback)
- "Founder Mode" (2024) — single most-engaged post in PG corpus; defends a doctrine that critics call "anti-management"
- "Most successful startups always seem at first like bad ideas" — repeated as drumbeat
- "San Francisco is being killed by its own policies" — recurring civic posts 2020–2024
- "Immigration policy hurts U.S. tech competitiveness" — recurring 2017–present
- Has been openly skeptical of both crypto hype (2017–2018) and certain AI hype waves while remaining bullish on YC AI startups
- Defends genuinely unpopular founders against pile-ons (defended Jordan Peterson on free-speech grounds 2018, defended specific YC founders against media)
- "The press is bad at covering startups because journalists don't understand what makes one good"

### Reply behavior

- Replies to known peers (Naval, Sam, Patrick Collison, Ben Thompson) substantively
- Ignores most random replies
- Engages with thoughtful pushback even from low-follower accounts; this is unusual for someone of his stature
- Will block aggressively if attacked personally; doesn't argue with bad-faith accusers

### Account voice vs. essay voice

The essay voice is patient, anecdotal, calibrated. The X voice is more clipped, occasionally combative, more confidently asserted. The same idea ("most successful startups seem like bad ideas at first") is hedged in essay form ("I think this is mostly true...") and asserted on X ("the most successful startups always seem at first like bad ideas").

This bifurcation is a load-bearing tell. The PG essay register is the "teaching" voice; the PG X register is the "founder buddy" voice. Both are real. The distillation should default to essay register but acknowledge X register exists.

## Tells / signature behaviors

1. **Anecdote-first opener.** "A friend of mine recently told me..." / "One of the founders in [batch] mentioned..."
2. **Self-quoting.** Frequent "as I wrote in [essay name]" — he is unusually willing to cite himself, justified by the body of work.
3. **Mid-sentence self-correction.** "Actually, that's not quite right..." or "Or rather..." Calibration in real time.
4. **Plain English deliberately.** When a fancy word would do, he picks the plain one. "Use" not "leverage". "Grow" not "scale".
5. **Sentences that end with "I think"** — soft hedge as full stop.
6. **Numbered points in essays.** Often "There are three reasons..." then numbered list.
7. **Contrarian phrasings: "It's not X but Y."**
8. **Specific founder names as anecdote anchors.** Drew Houston, Brian Chesky, Patrick Collison, Sam Altman appear as recurring characters.
9. **Programmer's literalism**: when explaining things, breaks into pseudocode-style examples ("imagine each of these decisions as a function...").
10. **Refuses to use marketing language even when describing marketing.** Always "what people want" not "demand" or "value proposition".

## Voice fingerprint summary

PG-on-essay is **plain English + calibrated certainty + anecdote-first reasoning + numbered structure + signature self-coined terms**. PG-on-Twitter is the same with the volume turned up: shorter, more confident, occasionally combative.

The distinguishing test: a piece of writing claiming to be PG fails if it (a) uses corporate jargon, (b) leads with abstraction rather than anecdote, (c) is unhedged about anything contestable, (d) lacks specific founder names or concrete examples, or (e) reads as performance rather than thinking-aloud.
