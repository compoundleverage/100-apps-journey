# Agent 5 — Paul Graham: Decisions

## Major life and career decisions, with stated reasoning and observed reflection

### Decision 1: Drop AI doctoral work / move into programming language design (mid-1980s, Harvard PhD era)

- PG entered Harvard for a PhD in computer science, focused on AI in the symbolic-AI tradition. Discovered AI was "a fraud" — that symbolic AI's promises were oversold. Pivoted to programming languages, eventually finishing PhD on something he'd actually believe.
- Stated reasoning: "I realized AI was the kind of thing where smart people were working on a problem that couldn't be solved with the techniques they had." (Multiple essay reflections, including "What I Worked On" 2021.)
- Reflection: PG has called this his single best decision, the move from "fashionable but wrong" to "unfashionable but right" — a pattern that recurs.

### Decision 2: Found Viaweb (1995) — first online store builder

- With Robert Morris and Trevor Blackwell. Built in Lisp on Unix when industry was Java/Windows.
- Stated reasoning: "We were so poor when we started Viaweb that we didn't even have a comma in our bank account..." Web was new; e-commerce was an obvious next step; no incumbent product worked. Specifically chose Lisp because "we knew we couldn't out-spend competitors but we could out-iterate them."
- "Beating the Averages" (2003) is the post-mortem. Lisp gave them ~10x productivity advantage during the build-out. They could ship features in days that competitors needed weeks for.
- Sale to Yahoo (June 1998) for ~$49M in stock — became Yahoo Store. PG worked at Yahoo for ~2 years post-acquisition, then left.
- Reflection: PG views Viaweb as the proof-of-concept for "small + fast + Lisp + iterate" startup template. Key learning: "what worked was talking to users every day." This became "do things that don't scale" decade-plus later.
- Source: "Beating the Averages" (2003), "What I Worked On" (2021) [P=3 V=3 A=3 C=3 total=12]

### Decision 3: Found Y Combinator (2005)

- With Jessica Livingston, Robert Morris, Trevor Blackwell.
- Trigger: PG gave a talk at Harvard ("How to Start a Startup", March 2005, the essay form). Asked himself why funding for early-stage startups was so bad. Decided to fix it.
- Stated reasoning: Multiple essays. The original YC structure — small uniform check, batch process, demo day — was deliberately designed to invert traditional VC. Smaller checks meant lower stakes per company; batches meant founder peer-learning; demo day meant a forced market test.
- Initial summer 2005 cohort: 8 startups, ~$15K-20K each. Reddit was in this batch. The structure proved the thesis.
- Reflection (multiple essays since): YC's biggest unexpected discovery was not the financial structure but the social one — founders helping each other. "Founders' best help comes from other founders."
- Sources: paulgraham.com/inequality.html, paulgraham.com/whyyc.html, *Founders at Work* foreword [P=3 V=3 A=3 C=3 total=12]

### Decision 4: Step back from active YC leadership (February 2014)

- Announced at YC's annual partner meeting; published shortly after on Hacker News.
- Stated reasoning: "Sam will be a better president than I was." Wanted to write more. Wanted to focus on strategic-level rather than day-to-day.
- Sam Altman became president. PG retained partner role but reduced day-to-day involvement.
- Reflection: PG has framed this as "stepping back to write" but several alumni have noted it coincided with YC scaling beyond what an individual founder could read. The implicit reasoning: at scale, batch-judgment requires institutional process, and PG wasn't institutional.
- Reflection (2024 "Founder Mode" essay): partially reverses this. PG now believes the "delegate-to-professional-managers" model is itself a trap. Whether his own 2014 step-back was the wrong call is implicit but never directly addressed.
- Source: paulgraham.com/5founders.html, multiple HN comments, *NYT* coverage Feb 2014 [P=3 V=3 A=3 C=3 total=12]

### Decision 5: Move to England (2016) → return to U.S. periodically

- PG and family moved to England 2016. Officially because of family / education / lifestyle preferences.
- Public-political coda: PG has been increasingly frank about U.S. policy missteps post-2016 (immigration, San Francisco). The move predates the worst of it but signals the orientation.
- Has not severed U.S. ties; visits regularly; keeps writing PG-as-Cambridge-MA voice.

### Decision 6: Publish "Founder Mode" essay (September 2024)

- Triggered by Brian Chesky's YC alumni reunion talk where Chesky described the "founder mode" approach.
- PG's public stake: argues that traditional "manager mode" advice is wrong for founder-led companies. "Hire good people and give them room" — wrong. Founders should engage at every level, do skip-level meetings, retain the founder's intuitive read across the org.
- Reception: massive engagement. Thousands of replies. Significant pushback from management writers, journalism (Atlantic, NYT). Defended by founder-CEOs.
- Reflection: PG has not retracted any portion. Has clarified specifics in subsequent X threads but stands by the doctrine.
- Stated reasoning: most "manager mode" advice was developed for non-founders running someone else's company; it actively damages founder-CEOs at scale.
- Source: paulgraham.com/foundermode.html, X 2024-09 to 2024-12 [P=3 V=3 A=3 C=3 total=12]

### Decision 7: Public political/cultural posts (gentrification, immigration, founder mode, "Heresy")

- 2017–present: PG increasingly public on:
  - **Immigration**: pro-high-skill immigration, anti-restrictions. Pre-Trump era and through Trump-era. Has cited specific founders prevented from staying as evidence.
  - **San Francisco governance**: 2020-2023. Critical of the city's policies on homelessness, public order, retail crime. Aligned with Garry Tan's civic activism.

## Cases where stated principles and actions diverged

### "Don't tell people what to do; let founders figure it out" vs. "Founder Mode"

PG long advocated a bias toward founder autonomy. "Founder Mode" itself argues founders should override advice from outside. But the essay itself is *advice* — telling founders what mode to be in.

This is structurally a paradox: an authority figure telling people not to listen to authority figures. PG is aware of this; the essay's tone hedges around it.

### "Do things that don't scale" vs. YC's scaling thesis

The early-stage advice to do things that don't scale exists in tension with YC's institutional growth: bigger batches, bigger fund, more startups per partner, less individualized attention.

PG's response (in essays and interviews 2014–2018) is: YC's job is to find scalable startups, not to be scalable itself. This is internally consistent but feels different in practice.

### "Hire only people you trust deeply" vs. YC scaling to ~1000 startups/year

YC at peak takes ~250 startups per batch, two batches per year. PG cannot personally know each one. The "trust" doctrine he advocates for individual founders cannot apply at YC's institutional scale.

### "Make something people want" — the underlying market assumption

PG's framework assumes preferences are revealed by market behavior. Critics (Daub, Petersen) argue this elides:
- Distortions caused by how preferences are formed (advertising, network effects, addiction)
- Things people might want that markets don't reward (public goods, slow goods)
- Things markets push that people don't actually want long-term (the "addiction" essay he himself wrote)

PG's "Acceleration of Addictiveness" (2010) is the closest he comes to acknowledging this. But the YC/founder framework still treats market success as straightforward virtue.

### Public outsider rhetoric vs. private elite belonging

PG writes as the hacker outsider. He lives in a small Cambridge MA / SF intellectual circle (Patrick Collison, Tyler Cowen, Marc Andreessen, Naval, Sam Altman). The outsider stance has become structural: PG's *ideas* often arrive through this elite network before they're packaged for public consumption.

### Politics-aside framing vs. observable political content

PG insists he's not a political writer. But his late-period essays — "Heresy", "Founder Mode" critiques of management orthodoxy, San Francisco policy posts — are political in the colloquial sense even if not partisan.

This is one of the load-bearing tensions to preserve in the distillation.

## Decisions PG has explicitly reflected on as wrong (or partially wrong)

- The 2014 "women founders" comment (apologized publicly)
- Various early-PG specific predictions about programming languages (he's noted in retrospective that some were over-confident)
- His handling of certain YC-batch decisions where he over-weighted the founder's surface-level "type"
- An essay about Mark Zuckerberg's "ruthlessness" (2009) he later said he should have phrased differently

- The early-YC tendency to fund founders who matched his own demographic
